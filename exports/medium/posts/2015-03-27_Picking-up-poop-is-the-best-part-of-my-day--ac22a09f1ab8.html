Picking up poop is the best part of my day.

Well, maybe not the actual poop part, but the rest of the job is fun.

At the wee age of 14, I was asked by one of my neighbors to go into her backyard once a week and throw a ball around for one of her three dogs. It was fun, and I had a dog of my own to keep me company. Despite that, I hadn’t truly fallen in love with dogs yet.

A year later, I was walking two of her dogs every weekday, and making a fair amount of money for a high school freshman. With $50 dollars a week in my pocket, I was killing it with the ladies. However, I had no idea about the lucrative business that was dog walking, especially in big cities. I was currently living in Poulsbo, a small town on the wrong side of the Puget Sound. With a population of a little over 9,000, I didn’t have very many opportunities to walk dogs for my neighbors, and most people who owned dogs were a four-person family, which had at least one stay-at-home parent.

When I moved to Seattle for school, I needed money to pay for the enormous tuition from SPU. I had two years of dog walking experience, so why not make a little business and start advertising? I talked to the managers at several apartment complexes and put some flyers in the lobbies. Now I had to wait.

It didn’t take too long to get my first client, a chocolate lab puppy named Hoosier. It was then that I finally discovered my love for all canine companions.

All this backstory, and I’m finally getting to the point. I can’t remember a day when I got frustrated with my job, or dreaded taking the time to let a dog out. Often, the best part of my day is getting to walk dogs. I can spend some time outside, walk around, and have fun with excited furballs, each full of personality and energy.

I’ve only had a few other jobs, all of which were part-time. I worked as a bagger at a grocery store. My task was to put items into a bag (very difficult work) and hand it to an often-grumpy customer who wouldn’t thank me most of the time. I enjoyed working with the people who were nice, and most of the employees were great, but that job saved me from getting a career in customer service.

Another job that I still currently hold is a Product Specialist for Suitable Technologies. My job is to sit in my room and use the Beam software to log into a Beam device at some event across the country. It’s such a novel experience to drive the devices, and people are often in awe of the technology. However, I find that the job can get dull at times, and it can be hard keeping a smile on your face for so many hours in a row.

I think that the true magic from dog walking is that the subject of my work lives and breathes and loves me just as much as I love it. I am willing to pick up its poop with my hands, because I feel as if the dog would do anything for me in return.

When bagging groceries, I don’t care about the organic, gluten-free, ultra-low-fat tofu that you bought, just like you don’t care about being snippy to me when you want double bags. When I’m working with people through the Beam Telepresence system, I care about the experience the other person has, but they couldn’t care less about what they say to me. They just want to see the device in action.

I wonder what other careers people might enjoy if there was a relationship like the one between a dog and its master. Is there a way we can translate that relationship into technology, where the user feels loved, and they care for the tool in the same way?
